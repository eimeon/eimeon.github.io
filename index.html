WEEK 1 - IDEATION

During the first week, I thought about something I really wanted to do and possible with our constraints. Fashion design has been one of my oldest dream, and although I did not get the chance to pursue it, this class might be the opportunity I was hoping for.

STEP 1

The first step consisted of researching designs made of 3d printed modular elements on the website Open Source Circular Fashion.

STEP 2

The second step consisted of putting on paper designs I liked and wanted to create such as those in the picture.

WEEK 2 - IDEATION & PROTOTYPING

During the second week, I pushed further the idea of the previous week to create an outfit for a festival. I spend time sketching different ideas. I was really inspired by the butterfly which represents a colorful but peaceful beauty of nature. In addition, I thought about the modulable part of the design and printed the first prototype of a module.

STEP 1

The first step consisted in a long and fastidious sketching phase where I really putted all my creativity to create the final design. After numerous sketches, I settled for the design that can be seen in the picture.

PANELS

The top is composed of 8 different and modulable panels shaped like small drops.

STEP 2

The next step was about finding an idea of how the texture of the design should be. I would like the texture to be small flowers with eventually butterfly on tops. However there is one constraint. As this design is made to be wearable, the small modules can not have sharp angles in direct contact of the skin.

MODULES

With the constraint of having no sharp angles in mind, I thought of multiple options. I wanted to keep the idea of tiny flowers intermodulable but binding them together with no extra element seemed impossible. However, it was possible to bind them with disks. The idea is that each disk will bind three modules together.

STEP 3

Step 3 was about creating a viable 3D-design for the modular part. For this part, I used the software Shapr3D, which is an intuitive 3D modelisation software made to make the design of 3D object for printing easier. I created this snowflake like shape based on the previous sketch of the modular element, as well as a disks with a small slot to try inserting the snowflake in. Here the height of the snowflake is 0.50mm to assure that it stays as bendable as possible.

3D PRINT

Here is a picture of the actual 3D print of the module. I printed with 2 layers of 0.30mm to save time even thought my model was 0.50mm. For that reason, the print of the disk, and more precisely the slot of the disk did not succeed. For next week, I want to test different heights for the snowflake and the disk and different slicing.

WEEK 5 - REDESIGN

After the non successful trial of week 2, I decided to redesign the whole project, learning from the mistakes of the past and drawing inspiration from the last weeks.

STEP 1 - IDEATION

The first step consisted of collecting ideas of a design that I would like to do. After spending sick days watching the Queen's Gambit, 'I was really inspired by vintage dresses design. As for the modules, I was inspired by the artist "sewprinted" on Instagram, and in particular this dress she made.

STEP 2 - 3D MODEL

The second step consisted of creating a 3D module based on the flat design from the previous step. This part was tedious as the module is very thin and therefore very breakable.

STEP 3 - TESTING

I printed the aforementioned design and realised that it was too thick (1mm). Therefore, I changed the thickness to 0.6mm. However, the modulable part seemed to work but I needed another test.

STEP 4 - RETESTING

With a thiner height, the module worked according to my prediction. I was really satisfied with the result and started printing batches of the same module.

STEP 5 - ASSEMBLING

After the first batches of the module were printed, I could start assembling the modules together to create the beginning of the rock.

WEEK 6 - PRINTING & ASSEMBLING

Week 6 was very intense. I spend 5 out of 5 days at the Fablab and printed in total around 10 batches. I assembled them and as I saw the growing number of students arriving to the Fablab to use the printers, I realised I will not be able to finish this project. However, I stayed motivated and stayed there as much as I could. My plan was to start from the middle of the skirt and work down from there as the details are the endings. As I realised that I will not be able to finish even that, I thought about printing only the front of the dress. As of the end of week 6, I highly doubt even this will be possible. Indeed, there are only 2 available printers for around 20 students. Because of the creative design exhibition, and two strike days in a row, we lost 3 out of the 6 classes. So this week, there is always at least 10 different people in the Fablab with 1h print each. With 2 printers, there is around a 5 hour waiting time for each print. In my case, a print covers a small surface of 10x5cm. Rare are the days where I can start 2 prints.

STEP 1 - WAITING WAITING WAITING

As mentioned earlier, I spend most of week 6 waiting for a printer to be free.

STEP 2 - PRINTING PRINTING PRINTING

I did have the possibility to print much more than expected. However, not enough to create a full skirt.

STEP 3 - ASSEMBLING ASSEMBLING ASSEMBLING

As I left the Fablab on Friday, this was as many pieces I had managed to assemble together. My phone camera broke a few days before and I was the only one left on the Fablab on Friday at 8:30pm, so the image is not of the best quality, but it shoes the state of the project at this moment of time.

STEP 4 - FINDING A MODEL AND A PHOTOGRAPH

During a party on a week end, I met with a make-up artist friend. I asked her to model the dress before knowing the dress would not be finished. I also had in mind to ask my roomate to take pictures with his analog camera to stay in the vintage vibe. All of these ideas wame before I knew the project would not be done. As of now, I cancelled everything and will model the small patch of the dress on me and on campus, with a normal iPhone camera.

WEEK 7 - FINAL TOUCH

For the last week, I wanted to print as many tinier module as possible to stay as close to the original design as possible. I finally could print 2 batches of the tinier module (in total 42 pieces).

FINAL ASSEMBLING

I arrived in the Fablab on Monday, ready to finish the skirt with the material I had already printed. The weather was beautiful so I took all the pieces outside for them and me to see the sun and take some fresh air.

I was working in front of the ENS building as people started to go outside to eat lunch. I continued assembling for multiple hours in the company of ENS students. After assembling the bigger pieces, I had trouble with positioning the smaller ones. As I had only a limited amount of the smaller pieces (42), I needed to calculate exactly how many I could put. I had enough to fit bigger 22 pieces on the last row. It should have been 23 but the last piece broke. I decided to do one row of 22 thiner pieces and then patches of 4 thiner pieces to give a spiky effect at the end of the skirt.
